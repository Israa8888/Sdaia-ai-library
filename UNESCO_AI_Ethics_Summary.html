International Standard
The first and only global normative framework on AI ethics, unanimously adopted by all 193 UNESCO Member States in November 2021. It sets universal values, principles, and policy actions to guide ethical AI development and deployment worldwide — including in Saudi Arabia and the GCC.
Overview

The UNESCO Recommendation on the Ethics of AI is a landmark international instrument adopted at UNESCO's 41st General Conference in November 2021. As a formal Recommendation under UNESCO's mandate, it does not constitute a legally binding treaty, but carries strong normative authority: Member States are expected to implement its provisions through national laws, policies, and governance structures, and to report their progress to UNESCO.


The document does not define AI narrowly. It describes AI systems as processing data in ways that simulate intelligent behaviour — spanning machine learning, deep learning, natural language processing, computer vision, planning, and optimisation — across all lifecycle stages from design and development through deployment to end-of-life disposal.


Its architecture follows a clear three-tier logic: 4 core values anchor the framework; 11 principles translate those values into operational ethics commitments; and 11 policy areas specify the concrete domains where governments must act.

Values are the aspirational ideals that inspire human conduct. Principles translate values into actionable ethics commitments. Policy Areas specify the concrete domains where governments must act. All three tiers are inseparable — the document must be read as a single whole.

The 4 Core Values — القيم الجوهرية الأربع
1 Human Dignity & Human Rights — كرامة الإنسان وحقوقه وحرياته الأساسية
Human dignity — which must never be violated and which every person must enjoy — is the foundation of the universal, indivisible, and inalienable system of human rights. AI systems across their entire lifecycle must respect, protect, and promote human dignity and rights in accordance with international law. Dignity is tied to recognising the inherent self-worth of every person regardless of race, colour, descent, sex, age, language, religion, political opinion, national or ethnic origin, economic status, disability, or any other ground. No individual or group may be subjected to any form of harm, humiliation, or degradation — physical, psychological, cultural, political, social, or economic — at any stage of an AI system's lifecycle. AI systems should be used to improve quality of life, leaving the definition of "quality of life" to individuals and communities, provided that definition does not violate human rights.
2 Environment & Ecosystems — ازدهار البيئة والنظم الإيكولوجية
The environment and ecosystems are existential necessities for humans and all living beings — without them, no AI progress can be enjoyed. This value must be considered throughout the entire AI lifecycle. All actors involved in AI must comply with international law and national legislation aimed at protecting, restoring, and sustaining the environment, including precautionary standards. AI systems must minimise their environmental footprint — including carbon emissions — and prevent unsustainable exploitation of natural resources that contributes to environmental degradation. Climate change risks, biodiversity loss, and ecosystem damage must be actively managed across AI design, training, deployment, and disposal stages.
3 Diversity & Inclusiveness — ضمان التنوع والشمول
Diversity and inclusion must be respected, protected, and promoted throughout AI lifecycles in accordance with international human rights law. This means actively promoting participation of all individuals and groups regardless of race, colour, descent, sex, age, language, religion, political opinion, national or ethnic origin, economic or social status, disability, or any other ground. AI systems must not restrict choices of lifestyle, beliefs, opinions, expression, or personal experiences at any lifecycle stage. Special efforts — including through international cooperation — must be made to overcome lack of infrastructure, education, technical skills, and legal frameworks, particularly in low- and middle-income countries, landlocked developing countries, and small island developing states.
4 Peaceful, Just & Interconnected Societies — العيش في مجتمعات مسالمة وعادلة ومترابطة
AI actors must work to ensure peaceful, just societies and create the conditions that enable people to live in them. Human interconnectedness means every person belongs to a community, and communities thrive when all members can thrive. This requires an organic, immediate sense of solidarity — a constant striving for peaceful relationships and care for others and the natural environment. AI lifecycle processes must not separate humans from communities, treat them as objects, undermine their freedom or integrity, or divide groups and incite conflict. AI systems must support the coexistence of humans, other living beings, and the natural environment — not threaten it.
The 11 Ethical Principles — المبادئ الأخلاقية الأحد عشر
Principle 1 — Proportionality & Do No Harm
AI systems must not exceed what is necessary to achieve legitimate purposes. Risk assessment procedures must be applied whenever harm to humans, human rights, communities, or ecosystems is possible. The choice to use AI and the choice of which AI tool must be justified: the tool must be fit for purpose, must not violate core values, and must be context-appropriate. The final decision on matters with irreversible consequences — or matters of life and death — must remain with humans. Mass surveillance use of AI is explicitly prohibited.
Principle 2 — Safety & Security
Unintended harms (safety risks) and vulnerabilities to attacks (security risks) must be avoided, mitigated, and addressed throughout the AI lifecycle to ensure the safety and security of humans and ecosystems. AI systems must be robust against adversarial attacks and data breaches. Testing, ongoing monitoring, and incident response mechanisms are required at every stage.
Principle 3 — Fairness & Non-discrimination
AI actors must not engage in unfair discrimination on any grounds. Benefits and costs of AI must be shared equitably. Particular attention is required for marginalised and vulnerable groups — including workers, children, elderly persons, persons with disabilities, and patients. Strategies to prevent, identify, and mitigate bias must be built into both training data and algorithm design.
Principle 4 — Sustainability
AI technologies do not by themselves guarantee the flourishing of human societies, the environment, or ecosystems. AI must be assessed against its impact on sustainability goals across social, political, environmental, educational, scientific, and economic dimensions. Environmental impact — particularly carbon footprint and resource consumption — must be systematically managed. AI must contribute to, not undermine, the UN Sustainable Development Goals.
Principle 5 — Right to Privacy & Data Protection
Privacy and data protection must be respected throughout the AI lifecycle. Data governance frameworks must ensure appropriate consent, access controls, purpose limitation, and the right to withdraw consent. States may regulate data generated or passing through their territories in accordance with international law, and must protect privacy as a human right. AI training must minimise use of personal or sensitive data. Clear, accessible information must be provided to data subjects on how their data is used.
Principle 6 — Human Oversight & Determination
Humans must retain the ability to oversee, intervene in, and shut down AI systems when they do not operate as intended. Decisions with irreversible or life-or-death consequences must remain under human determination, not AI. AI governance structures must define appropriate human oversight mechanisms with clear accountability, key performance indicators, and intervention strategies. Excessive trust in or dependence on AI systems must be actively prevented by design.
Principle 7 — Transparency & Explainability
The level of transparency and explainability must be appropriate to the context and risk level. Transparency means that information about AI systems is available and accessible. Explainability means that the reasoning behind AI decisions can be communicated to users and affected parties in clear, non-technical language. End users must always know they are interacting with an AI system, not a human. Transparency is a prerequisite for accountability and is inseparable from trustworthiness.
Principle 8 — Responsibility & Accountability
Ethical and legal responsibility for AI-based decisions and actions must always be traceable to AI actors in accordance with their role in the lifecycle. It must always be possible to assign ethical responsibility to a specific actor. Oversight mechanisms, impact assessments, audit trails, and whistleblower protections must be in place. Technical and institutional measures must ensure AI systems are auditable and traceable — especially to identify and address human rights violations and threats to ecosystems.
Principle 9 — Awareness & AI Literacy
Awareness and literacy in AI technologies and data value must be promoted among the general public through open and accessible education, civic engagement, digital skills training, AI ethics training, and media and information literacy. Efforts must account for linguistic, social, and cultural diversity to ensure effective public participation. All members of society must be able to make informed decisions about their use of AI systems and to be protected from undue influence. AI literacy programmes must be cross-sectoral — governments, international organisations, civil society, private sector, academia, media, and community leaders must all participate.
Principle 10 — Multi-stakeholder & Adaptive Governance
International law and national sovereignty must be respected in AI data governance. AI governance must be multi-stakeholder, multi-disciplinary, and pluralistic — involving governments, international organisations, civil society, private sector, academia, and affected communities. Governance must be adaptive: it must evolve with technology and must be able to address new challenges as they emerge. National AI ethics committees and robust regulatory frameworks are recommended mechanisms. Governance structures must align with the ethics governance approach proposed in the national AI ethics principles.
Principle 11 — Peaceful & Beneficial Use
AI technologies must be used for peaceful purposes and to benefit humanity, individuals, communities, and ecosystems. Their use in armed conflict must comply with international humanitarian law. AI systems must not be used to threaten international peace and security, undermine state sovereignty, or facilitate oppression. Benefits must be shared equitably, with special attention to the needs and contributions of low- and middle-income countries. The digital divide — in infrastructure, education, skills, and legal frameworks — must be actively addressed to ensure no country or person is left behind.
11 Policy Action Areas
Area 1
Ethical Impact Assessment
Governments must develop EIA methodologies for AI systems before and during deployment.
Area 2
Ethical Governance & Oversight
Establishing national AI ethics bodies, regulatory frameworks, and oversight mechanisms.
Area 3
Data Policies
Data governance, protection, access equity, and lifecycle management for AI-related data.
Area 4
Development & International Cooperation
Bridging the AI divide between developed and developing nations; technology transfer.
Area 5
Environment & Ecosystems
Reducing environmental footprint of AI; supporting sustainable AI infrastructure.
Area 6
Gender Equality
Eliminating gender bias in AI systems; ensuring equitable female participation in AI.
Area 7
Culture
Protecting cultural heritage and linguistic diversity; preventing AI-driven cultural homogenisation.
Area 8
Education, Research & Science
AI literacy for all; ethics integration in STEM and social science education.
Area 9
Communication & Information
Tackling disinformation, algorithmic bias in media, and access to trustworthy information.
Area 10
Economy & Labour
Managing AI impact on jobs; ensuring fair transition and worker protection.
Area 11
Health & Social Well-being
Ethical AI in healthcare; ensuring AI promotes rather than undermines well-being.
Authority & Legal Basis
Issuing Body: UNESCO — United Nations Educational, Scientific and Cultural Organization.

Legal Status: A formal Recommendation under UNESCO's mandate — not a binding treaty, but carries strong normative authority. Member States are expected to report on implementation to UNESCO every four years.

Basis: Rooted in the UN Charter, international human rights law, the UN SDGs, the 2019 UNESCO COMEST Preliminary Study on AI Ethics, and UNESCO Declarations on cultural diversity, bioethics, and the information society.

Key Obligation: UNESCO will develop a methodology for Ethical Impact Assessments (EIA) of AI systems and will assist Member States in readiness assessments across scientific, economic, legal, regulatory, and cultural dimensions.

Implications for ICAIRE Research
→
Ethical Impact Assessment (EIA) is a UNESCO obligation. Any AI research, tool, or project that ICAIRE supports or publishes should be evaluated through an EIA lens. ICAIRE can develop and publish EIA methodologies tailored to the Saudi/GCC context — a high-value research gap.
→
AI literacy and education research (Policy Area 8) is explicitly prioritised by UNESCO. Studies on AI education, workforce readiness, and public understanding align directly with UNESCO priorities and are highly fundable at the international level.
→
Gender equality in AI (Policy Area 6) is a UNESCO-required policy emphasis. Research that examines gender bias in AI systems or female participation in AI development is explicitly supported by this Recommendation.
→
Data governance research (Policy Area 3) is a strategic priority. ICAIRE can publish comparative research on how Saudi Arabia's national data policies align or diverge from UNESCO norms.
→
Saudi Arabia's compliance journey with this Recommendation — how SDAIA's frameworks align with UNESCO norms — is itself a valid and important research topic for ICAIRE to explore and publish on globally.
→
Citation authority: This is the highest-level international reference for AI ethics. Every ICAIRE publication discussing ethics should cite this Recommendation as the global normative baseline, alongside SDAIA's national frameworks.
