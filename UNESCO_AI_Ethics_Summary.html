International Standard
The first and only global normative framework on AI ethics, unanimously adopted by all 193 UNESCO Member States in November 2021. It sets universal values, principles, and policy actions to guide ethical AI development and deployment worldwide — including in Saudi Arabia and the GCC.
Overview

The UNESCO Recommendation on the Ethics of AI is a landmark international instrument adopted at UNESCO's 41st General Conference in November 2021. As a formal Recommendation under UNESCO's mandate, it does not constitute a legally binding treaty, but carries strong normative authority: Member States are expected to implement its provisions through national laws, policies, and governance structures, and to report their progress to UNESCO.


The document does not define AI narrowly. It describes AI systems as processing data in ways that simulate intelligent behaviour — spanning machine learning, deep learning, natural language processing, computer vision, planning, and optimisation — across all lifecycle stages from design and development through deployment to end-of-life disposal.


Its architecture follows a clear three-tier logic: 4 core values anchor the framework; 11 principles translate those values into operational ethics commitments; and 11 policy areas specify the concrete domains where governments must act.

Area 1
Ethical Impact Assessment
Governments must develop EIA methodologies for AI systems before and during deployment.
Area 2
Ethical Governance & Oversight
Establishing national AI ethics bodies, regulatory frameworks, and oversight mechanisms.
Area 3
Data Policies
Data governance, protection, access equity, and lifecycle management for AI-related data.
Area 4
Development & International Cooperation
Bridging the AI divide between developed and developing nations; technology transfer.
Area 5
Environment & Ecosystems
Reducing environmental footprint of AI; supporting sustainable AI infrastructure.
Area 6
Gender Equality
Eliminating gender bias in AI systems; ensuring equitable female participation in AI.
Area 7
Culture
Protecting cultural heritage and linguistic diversity; preventing AI-driven cultural homogenisation.
Area 8
Education, Research & Science
AI literacy for all; ethics integration in STEM and social science education.
Area 9
Communication & Information
Tackling disinformation, algorithmic bias in media, and access to trustworthy information.
Area 10
Economy & Labour
Managing AI impact on jobs; ensuring fair transition and worker protection.
Area 11
Health & Social Well-being
Ethical AI in healthcare; ensuring AI promotes rather than undermines well-being.
Authority & Legal Basis
Issuing Body: UNESCO — United Nations Educational, Scientific and Cultural Organization.

Legal Status: A formal Recommendation under UNESCO's mandate — not a binding treaty, but carries strong normative authority. Member States are expected to report on implementation to UNESCO every four years.

Basis: Rooted in the UN Charter, international human rights law, the UN SDGs, the 2019 UNESCO COMEST Preliminary Study on AI Ethics, and UNESCO Declarations on cultural diversity, bioethics, and the information society.

Key Obligation: UNESCO will develop a methodology for Ethical Impact Assessments (EIA) of AI systems and will assist Member States in readiness assessments across scientific, economic, legal, regulatory, and cultural dimensions.

Implications for ICAIRE Research
→
Ethical Impact Assessment (EIA) is a UNESCO obligation. Any AI research, tool, or project that ICAIRE supports or publishes should be evaluated through an EIA lens. ICAIRE can develop and publish EIA methodologies tailored to the Saudi/GCC context — a high-value research gap.
→
AI literacy and education research (Policy Area 8) is explicitly prioritised by UNESCO. Studies on AI education, workforce readiness, and public understanding align directly with UNESCO priorities and are highly fundable at the international level.
→
Gender equality in AI (Policy Area 6) is a UNESCO-required policy emphasis. Research that examines gender bias in AI systems or female participation in AI development is explicitly supported by this Recommendation.
→
Data governance research (Policy Area 3) is a strategic priority. ICAIRE can publish comparative research on how Saudi Arabia's national data policies align or diverge from UNESCO norms.
→
Saudi Arabia's compliance journey with this Recommendation — how SDAIA's frameworks align with UNESCO norms — is itself a valid and important research topic for ICAIRE to explore and publish on globally.
→
Citation authority: This is the highest-level international reference for AI ethics. Every ICAIRE publication discussing ethics should cite this Recommendation as the global normative baseline, alongside SDAIA's national frameworks.
